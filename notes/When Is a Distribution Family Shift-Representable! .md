## When Is a Distribution Family Shift-Representable? 

Signal detection theory (SDT; Egan, 1958; Green & Swets, 1966) has long provided the dominant formal language for recognition memory and related judgment tasks. In its simplest form, SDT assumes that studied and new items give rise to latent strength values on a continuous internal scale, and that responses are generated by comparing those values against one or more decision criteria. In practice, however, this core idea is almost always supplemented by auxiliary assumptions about the form of the latent distributions, most commonly Gaussian ones, and empirical debates have therefore tended to proceed by asking which particular parametric model best fits observed receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves.

Recent work has challenged the adequacy of that strategy. Rouder and colleagues (2015) emphasize that the bare core of continuous SDT is, by itself, too unconstrained to be genuinely testable: if signal and noise distributions are allowed to take arbitrary forms, then any ROC curve can be generated by some pair of latent distributions. In that sense, unrestricted SDT is unfalsifiable, and apparent empirical support for one “signal detection model” over another may simply reflect arbitrary auxiliary choices rather than substantive theoretical commitments. Kellen, Winiger, Dunn, and Singmann (2021) make the same point in more general terms by distinguishing theory from model and arguing that informative tests must target properties that hold across families of SDT models rather than features tied to a single parametric instantiation.

Such concerns have motivated a resurgence in a structuralist approach (e.g. Krantz et al., 1971) to the analysis of choice data in multiple domains (Birnbaum, 2008; Kellen et al, 2021; Luce, 2010; Regenwetter, Dana and Davis-Stober, 2011). Instead of comparing isolated parametric fits, these approaches derive formal consequences of broad assumptions and test those consequences directly. For example, in recognition memory, Kellen et al. develop this program explicitly, organizing SDT models by increasingly specific structural properties—random-scale representation, latent-variable independence, likelihood-ratio monotonicity, ROC asymmetry, and nonthreshold representation—and then deriving experimentally testable constraints associated with each. Their work shows how much of the theoretical content of SDT can be articulated without committing in advance to Gaussian, logistic, or other conventional distributional forms.

Within this tradition, Rouder et al. proposed that continuous SDT models should satisfy a further simplicity constraint: **shift-representability**. At the ROC level, a family is shift-representable if there exists a common monotone transformation that linearizes all curves into lines of common slope that differ only in intercept; at the latent level, this is equivalent to requiring that the condition-specific strength distributions can be expressed as shifts of a single underlying distribution. Rouder’s argument was that shift-representability restores to SDT the kind of lawful simplicity it was originally meant to capture, without reintroducing psychologically empty parametric assumptions. 

Dunn and colleagues (2026) endorse this proposal for a related, measurement-theoretic reason: a shift-representable SDT model decomposes into an additive linear component and a monotone measurement component, and therefore instantiates an additive conjoint measurement structure (Luce & Tukey, 1964). On this view, if familiarity is to function as a scientifically useful quantity, then additivity is not merely mathematically convenient but measurement-theoretically central.

Once shift-representability is taken seriously, however, a new question arises - how do we know if a collection of random variables is shift-representable? Existing methods are aimed primarily at empirical data: Rouder’s spline-based procedure asks whether a family of observed ROC curves admits a common linearizing transformation, while Dunn’s monotonic linear regression (mLR) approach asks whether observed rating data are consistent with some shift-representable latent model and, if so, what latent distribution best recovers that structure. Those are powerful tools for testing whether a shift-representable account is compatible with data. But they do not directly answer a different, more analytic question: given a specific family of distributions, how can one determine whether it is shift-representable in the first place? 

This question is already implicit in contemporary debates. For example, Dunn argues that the REM model of recognition memory (Shiffrin & Steyvers, 1997) is shift-representable because data simulated from REM are well captured by the mLR framework. But if the relevant latent distributions were known explicitly, could one establish shift-representability directly from their distribution functions, without appealing to simulated data or numerical recovery?

The present paper addresses that question broadly construed. Although the motivating application is SDT and recognition memory, the target property is a general mathematical property of distributional families: whether an arbitrary collection of random variables can be transformed into a shift family by a single monotone reparameterization.

## 1\. Problem statement

Let $X=\{X_1,X_2,\dots,X_k\}$ be a finite family of random variables defined on a common latent scale. Assume that each $X_n$ has a continuous, strictly increasing cumulative distribution function $F_{X_n}:\mathbb R\to[0,1]$. For ease of notation, write $F_n\equiv F_{X_n}$.

### **Definition 1 (Shift-representability).**

The family $X$ is said to be **shift-representable** if there exists a strictly monotone function $g:\R\to\R$ such that the transformed random variables $Y_n=g(X_n)$ belong to a common shift family. That is, there exist a base distribution function $F_Y$ and constants $\mu_1,\dots,\mu_k\in\mathbb R$ such that

$$
F_{Y_n}(y)=F_Y(y-\mu_n) \qquad\text{for all }n=1,\dots,k. \tag{1.1}
$$

If so, then the family possesses a common additive latent structure, obscured only by the choice of measurement coordinate. If not, then no monotone regraduation of the scale can reduce the family to pure shifts.

The aim of this paper is to characterize this property directly at the level of the distribution functions themselves. Existing methods in the literature address a neighboring but distinct question: given empirical ROC curves or empirical response distributions, are the data consistent with *some* shift-representable model? The core questions addressed here are:

1. **Existence.** What are the necessary and sufficient conditions under which there exists a strictly monotone transformation $g$ that makes the family $\{g(X_n)\}$ a shift family?

2. **Recognition.** How can those conditions be checked directly from the distribution functions $F_1,\dots,F_k$ or other distributional properties, without first fitting splines, simulating data, or appealing to a particular parametric embedding?

3. **Empirical consistency.** Which observable properties of empirical cdfs indicate compatibility with an underlying shift-representable family, and how do these relate to the analytic conditions above?

The central problem is therefore to determine when a finite family of continuous distributions is, up to a common monotone change of coordinate, a location family.

## 2\. Constraints on the relations among distribution functions via transport maps and the Abel-equation

Since distribution functions are invariant under strictly increasing reparameterizations, **Definition 1** of shift-representability of the family $X$ is equivalent to

$$
F_{n}(x)=F_Y(g(x)-\mu_n), \qquad n = 1, \dots , k. \tag{2.1}
$$

As it stands, however, this condition is not operational: it depends on the unknown transformation $g$, and if the family is *not* shift-representable, one would in principle have to rule out all possible monotone regraduations. We therefore seek conditions that can be formulated directly in terms of the cdfs $F_1,\dots,F_k$.

Fix $F_1$ as a reference distribution and define the associated **quantile-matching transport maps**

$$
\psi_n := F_1^{-1}\circ F_n : \mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}, \qquad n=1,\dots,k.
$$

where $F^{-1}: [0,1] \to \R$ refers to the inverse of the CDF (i.e. the quantile function). Because each $F_n$ is continuous and strictly increasing on $\mathbb{R}$, $F_n^{-1}$ are well-defined, each $\psi_n$ is an increasing homeomorphism of $\mathbb{R}$, and $\psi_1=\mathrm{id}$. 

Intuitively, $\psi_n$ is the unique monotone map that transports the quantiles of $X_n$ onto the scale of $X_1$. One such transport map, $\psi_2(x) = F_1^{-1}(F_2(x))$, where $X_1 \sim \Gamma(5,3)$ and $X_2 \sim \Gamma(5,2)$, is illustrated for two different values of $x$ in the left panel on Figure 1:

The key fact is that, if the family is shift-representable, then after applying the correct latent regraduation $g$, every transport map must become a simple horizontal translation (right panel). This immediately forces strong observable restrictions.

Focusing on the transport maps reframes the problem: instead of searching over all monotone $g$, we study algebraic/dynamical constraints on the *generated group* $G=\langle \psi_1,\dots,\psi_k\rangle$ under composition and inverse. The main structural facts are the following.

### Theorem 2: Structural conditions for shift-representability

Let $G:=\langle \psi_1,\dots,\psi_k\rangle$ denote the group generated by the transport maps under composition and inverses. Then the following three conditions are jointly necessary and sufficient for $X$ to be shift-representable:

- **(Part 1) Pairwise commutativity is necessary.**

   If $X$ is shift-representable, then

   $$
   \psi_n\circ\psi_m=\psi_m\circ\psi_n
   
   \qquad\text{for all }m,n.
   $$

- **(Part 2) Pairwise noncrossing is necessary.**

   If $X$ is shift-representable and

   $$
   
   \psi_n(x^*)=\psi_m(x^*)
   
   \quad\text{for some }x^*\in\mathbb R,
   
   $$

   then $\psi_n=\psi_m$. In particular, every nontrivial transport map has no fixed point.

- **(Part 3) A sufficient condition is that** $G$ **is disjoint, provided the induced action fills the line.**

   Suppose $G$ is **disjoint**, meaning that whenever $f,g\in G$ and $f(x^*)=g(x^*)$ for some $x^*\in\mathbb R$, then $f=g$. Suppose moreover that either

   - $G$ is cyclic, or

   - $G$ is noncyclic and $L(G)=\mathbb R$,

   where $L(G)$ denotes the common orbit-closure set of the group $G$. Then $X$ is shift-representable. The cyclic/noncyclic dichotomy and the role of $L(G)$ come from the simultaneous Abel-equation theory for disjoint groups of increasing interval bijections. 

**Figure 2** visualizes why commutativity is a natural necessary condition. In a shift-representable family, every transport map becomes a translation after the same monotone regraduation, and translations commute. Accordingly, in the Gamma example with fixed shape and varying rate, applying the transport maps in either order leads to the same endpoint. In the unequal-variance normal example, the two orderings lead to different endpoints, revealing a failure of commutativity and therefore ruling out shift-representability.

Before proving **Theorem 2**, we first note a structural equivalence that recasts the entire problem as a simultaneous Abel-equation system. This reformulation is exact, and it explains why the transport maps are the right objects to study.

### Proposition 3: Shift-representability is equivalent to a simultaneous Abel system

The family $X=\{X_1,\dots,X_k\}$ is shift-representable if and only if there exist a strictly increasing bijection $g:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ and constants $c_1,\dots,c_k\in\mathbb R$, with $c_1=0$, such that

$$
g(\psi_n(x))=g(x)+c_n

\qquad\text{for all }x\in\mathbb R, \; n=1,\dots,k.

\tag{2.2}
$$

Equivalently, recast in group action form:

$$
\psi_n = g^{-1}\circ \tau_{c_n}\circ g,

\qquad\text{where }\tau_c(y)=y+c.

\tag{2.3}
$$

Equation **2\.2** is precisely a system of simultaneous Abel functional equations, which have been extensively studied in iteration theory and functional analysis (Jarczyk et al., 1994; Farzadfard & Robati, 2012; Zdun, 1989).

#### Proof

***Necessity***. Assume first that $X$ is shift-representable. Using **Equation 2.1** and the definition of $\psi_n(x)= F_1^{-1}(F_n(x))$, we obtain

$$
F_Y\bigl(g(x)-\mu_n\bigr) =F_Y\bigl(g(\psi_n(x))-\mu_1\bigr).
$$

From here we apply $F_Y^{-1}$ to the first and last term, and rearrange the constants to get

$$

g(\psi_n(x))=g(x)+(\mu_1-\mu_n).
$$

Thus (2.2) holds with $c_n=\mu_1-\mu_n$.

***Sufficiency***. Conversely, suppose that there exist $g$ and constants $c_n$ such that (2.2) holds. Define the base CDF of $Y$ via the reference $F_1:$

$$

F_Y(y):=F_1(g^{-1}(y)).
$$

Then using this fact together with our earlier definitions, we find that

$$
F_n(x) 

= F_1(F_1^{-1}(F_n(x)))

=F_1(\psi_n(x))

=F_Y(g(\psi_n(x)))

=F_Y(g(x)+c_n).
$$

Writing $\mu_n:=-c_n$ gives

$$

F_n(x)=F_Y(g(x)-\mu_n),
$$

so the family is shift-representable. ∎

Proposition 1 shows that shift-representability is exactly the problem of finding a single increasing coordinate $g$ that simultaneously turns all transport maps into translations. In other words, the problem is no longer intrinsically probabilistic; it has become a problem in one-dimensional dynamics and functional equations. This is precisely the setting of the **simultaneous Abel equation**, studied classically for commuting interval homeomorphisms and, more generally, for disjoint groups of increasing bijections. 

We can now prove the first two parts of Theorem 2 directly.

### Proof of Theorem 2, parts 1 and 2

Assume that $X$ is shift-representable. By Proposition 3, there exists a strictly increasing bijection $g$ and constants $c_n$ such that

$$

\psi_n = g^{-1}\circ \tau_{c_n}\circ g,

\qquad \tau_{c_n}(y)=y+c_n.
$$

Part 1 (commutatitivity of transport maps) follows immediately, since translations commute:

$$

\begin{aligned}

\psi_n\circ\psi_m

&= g^{-1}\circ \tau_{c_n}\circ \tau_{c_m}\circ g \\

&= g^{-1}\circ \tau_{c_m}\circ \tau_{c_n}\circ g \\

&= \psi_m\circ\psi_n.

\end{aligned}
$$

Thus commutativity is an unavoidable first diagnostic. If even two of the quantile-matching maps fail to commute, no common monotone regraduation can turn the family into pure shifts.

For Part 2 (pairwise non-crossing), suppose that $\psi_n(x^*)=\psi_m(x^*)$ for some $x^*\in\mathbb R$. Applying $g$ gives

$$

g(x^*)+c_n = g(x^*)+c_m,
$$

hence $c_n=c_m$, and therefore $\psi_n=\psi_m$. Taking $m=1$ and using $\psi_1=\mathrm{id}$ yields the fixed-point corollary.

The logic behind both claims is simple: once the maps are conjugate to translations, they must inherit two visible signatures of translations--translations commute, and distinct translations never intersect.

It is worth noting, however, that Parts 1 and 2 are **not sufficient** by themselves. A family of transport maps may commute and be pairwise noncrossing, yet still fail to be shift-representable because a hidden oscillatory distortion appears only after iteration or composition (see Appendix A for an example). Thus the correct sufficient condition must constrain not only the original transport maps, but the entire group they generate.

### Why the generated group matters

The role of the generated group $G=\langle\psi_{1},...,\psi_{k}\rangle$ is exactly to capture these hidden higher-order relations. If the family were genuinely conjugate to translations, then not only the original maps, but every element obtained from them by composition, inverse, and iteration would also have to behave like a translation. This is why the right strengthening of pairwise noncrossing is disjointness of the whole generated group.

This structural property directly unlocks the analytic guarantee we need. Jarczyk, Loskot, and Zdun (1994) demonstrated that if a noncyclic group of interval homeomorphisms is disjoint, the corresponding simultaneous Abel system admits a continuous solution, unique up to an additive constant.

Farzadfard and Robati (2012) refined this by classifying the exact topological nature of that solution based on the group's orbit-closure, $L(G)$. For a noncyclic disjoint group, exactly two outcomes exist for the continuous solution:

1. **The Cantor-type case:** If $L(G)$ is a nowhere-dense set (a "spoiled" disjoint group), the continuous solution is a Cantor function. Because this function is constant on the components outside the limit set, it fails the strict monotonicity required by Definition 1. 

2. **The Homeomorphic case:** If the orbit-closure fills the entire scale, $L(G)=\mathbb{R}$, the continuous solution is guaranteed to be a true homeomorphism.

Therefore, specifying that $G$ is disjoint ensures a continuous transformation exists, and adding the condition $L(G)=\mathbb{R}$ guarantees this transformation is strictly increasing. This perfectly yields the required strictly monotone regraduation $g$ that simultaneously linearizes all transport maps, proving shift-representability.

### Proof of Theorem 2, part 3

Assume that the generated group $G$ is disjoint.

If $G$ is cyclic, then it is generated by a single increasing fixed-point-free homeomorphism of $\mathbb R$. Such a map is conjugate to a translation, and hence every element of $G$ is conjugate to a translation by the same increasing homeomorphism. By Proposition 1, the family is shift-representable.

Now suppose that $G$ is noncyclic and $L(G)=\mathbb R$. By the results of Jarczyk, Loskot, and Zdun (1996), refined and recast by Farzadfard and Robati (2012), the corresponding simultaneous Abel system admits a continuous solution $\phi$, unique up to affine normalization, and when $L(G)=\mathbb R$ this solution is in fact a homeomorphism. Equivalently, there exists a strictly increasing bijection $g:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$ such that every $f\in G$ has the form

$$

f = g^{-1}\circ \tau_{\lambda(f)}\circ g
$$

for some real number $\lambda(f)$. In particular, each generator $\psi_n$ has this form. Proposition 1 then implies that the original family $X$ is shift-representable.

This completes the proof of Theorem 2. ∎

### Remarks

- **Why Theorem 2 is phrased in this order.**

Parts 1 and 2 are the most transparent and empirically useful consequences: they depend only on the observed cdfs and can immediately rule out shift-representability. Proposition 1 explains *why* these conditions appear: they are simply the observable shadows of a hidden translation structure.

- **Why Part 3 is stronger than Parts 1 and 2.**

Pairwise commutativity and pairwise noncrossing constrain only the original listed transport maps. By contrast, disjointness of $G$ requires that **all** iterates, inverses, and compositions generated by them remain noncrossing. This is exactly the strengthening needed to eliminate oscillatory counterexamples.

- **Why the condition $L(G)=\mathbb R$ appears.**

In the noncyclic case, disjointness alone guarantees a continuous Abel coordinate, but not necessarily a one-to-one regraduation. The additional condition $L(G)=\mathbb R$ rules out the Cantor-type case and ensures that the Abel coordinate is a homeomorphism, which is exactly what shift-representability requires.
